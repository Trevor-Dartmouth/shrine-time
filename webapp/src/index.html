What are the “Sengen Shrines”?

One of the largest religious complexes in all of Japan and considered Important Cultural Property, Shizuoka Sengen Jinja serves as the collective name for twenty-six Shinto structures in Shizuoka Prefecture, including three main Shinto shrines: Kambe Jinja, Ohtoshimioya Jinja, and Sengen Jinja (1). The main matsuri for the complex, the Hatsukakai Festival, is held annually April 1-5 to celebrate Mt. Fuji (2).

Kambe Jinja

The primary kami of Kambe Jinja is Takami Omi, or Okuninushi, who is said to have founded the historical Suruga Province, now a part of the modern Shizuoka Prefecture (2). The shrine itself is believed to be 2,100 years old (3).

Ohtoshimioya Jinja

Over 1,700 years old, Ohtoshimioya Jinja is said to have been enshrined during the era of Emperor Ojin and is relatively simple in design compared to the other main shrines. The primary kami of Ohtoshimioya Jinja is Ukanotimama, a goddess of rice and prosperity (5).

Sengen Jinja

The Sengen Jinja is just as commonly referred to as the Asama Jinja, or “volcano shrine” for its role in veneration of Konohanasakuya-hime, the female kami of Mt. Fuji (2). The newest of the three main shrines, Sengen Jinja was built in 901 by Emperor Daigo (5).

Within the shine complex and located behind the main hall of Ohtoshimioya Jinja is a 150 foot hilltop housing a burial ground called Shizuhatayama Kofun. The dome-shaped burial ground, a traditional kofun, has been registered as a National Historic Site in Japan since 1953 and was built from Abe River boulders around the 6th century.

Documentation from the Edo period indicates that the tomb was discovered in the late-1700’s to early-1800’s after its surface was pierced by a fallen tree after a storm (6). Upon numerous surveys of the incredibly rare archaeological site, a stone sarcophagus was found, made out of volcanic teff likely originating from the nearby Izu peninsula.

Though the identity of the buried individual remains unknown, artifacts found within both the chamber and casket reveal a man of great wealth, with gold-copper jewelry, iron swords, and Sue ware from all over the region (7). These are available for viewing at the small museum within the complex (5).

Built for a Warrior

During the Kamakura and Muromachi Periods, Shizuoka Sengen Jinja was patroned notably by warrior clan families, particularly the Imagawa clan, who took hostage a young Tamagawa warrior by the name of Tokugawa Ieyasu and brought him to Shizuoka.

Tokugawa Ieyasu, having experienced his coming of age at Shizuoka Sengen Jinja, served as its patron after his retirement as Shogun, or feudal ruler, having overthrown the Imagawa. In honor of his grandfather, Tokugawa Ieyasu’s grandson third Shogun Iemitsu rebuilt a crumbling Shizuoka Sengen Jinja in 1634, only to have it burnt down again 150 years later.

The third renovation of the shrine complex, completed in 1865, took over sixty years of effort and a team of highly skilled tradesmen; today Shizuoka Sengen Jinja is a beautiful and celebratory reflection of classic Momoyama-style architecture, featuring intricate wood carvings, red lacquer, and gold leaf (8).
